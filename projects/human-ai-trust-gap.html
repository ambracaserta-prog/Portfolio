The Human–AI Trust Gap

A cross-cultural, mixed-methods study of how Italian and American consumers actually come to trust — or distrust — AI in fashion marketing. 176 survey respondents, 7 expert interviews, and three assumptions that didn't survive contact with the data.

AuthorAmbra Caserta
MethodSurvey (t-test / ANOVA / Pearson) + thematic interviews
Sample98 US · 78 Italy

01

The Challenge

AI is reshaping fashion marketing through hyper-personalization — but adoption is usually treated as a technical rollout problem, not a psychological and cultural one. I wanted to test, empirically, whether trust in AI actually differs across cultures and generations the way the industry assumes it does, and separate the trends that are real from the ones that are just widely repeated.

02

The Approach

A mixed-methods design, built to triangulate rather than assume:

Quant

176 survey responses

98 US, 78 Italy. Eight trust dimensions, four privacy dimensions, 7-point Likert scale. Analyzed with independent-samples t-tests, one-way ANOVA, and Pearson correlations in SPSS.

Qual

7 expert interviews

AI strategists, luxury brand leaders, consumer psychologists, and academics across US and Italian markets. Semi-structured, thematically coded (Braun & Clarke, 2006).

Scope

What this design can — and can't — claim

A convenience sample at a single point in time. Strong for surfacing directional insight and testing assumptions. Not built for causal or fully generalizable claims — treated accordingly throughout.

03

What the Data Actually Showed

One confirmed finding, two real-but-modest signals, and three popular assumptions that didn't hold up. Here's the confirmed one first — it's the strongest thing in the study.

Trust in AI's human-like decisions, by age group

F(5,165) = 2.945, p = .014 — the only trust dimension to reach statistical significance across any grouping in the study. Reproduced from thesis Figure 3.

Significant

The generational pivot

Ages 35–64 trusted AI's human-like decision-making significantly more than younger respondents — the opposite of the "digital native" narrative. "Silver Spenders" may be an underexplored segment for AI product design, not a deprioritized one.

p = .014 (ANOVA)
Weak, real signal

Trust is partly cognitive

Privacy- and ethics-sensitive respondents showed a small but significant tendency to better understand how AI works. Not a claim that they trust AI more overall — a signal that transparency may be an underused lever, worth testing further.

r = .202, p < .01 · r = .165, p < .05
Directional only

US–Italy: a trend, not a gap

Across 12 measured dimensions, zero reached conventional significance. Two trended in the theoretically expected direction — US slightly higher on AI-human equality, Italy slightly higher on privacy concern.

closest: p = .081 / p = .099

Diffusion of Innovation

Rogers, 1962

Explains adoption timing across a population by relative advantage, compatibility, and complexity. Framed the expectation of Italy as a more cautious "late majority" market.

Psychological Ownership

Pierce et al., 2003

Trust increases with control, effort, and investment in a process. Framed why participatory AI experiences might build more trust than fully automated ones.

Cultural Dimensions

Hofstede, 1980

Uncertainty avoidance and individualism vs. collectivism. Predicted US openness to AI experimentation and Italian caution — a prediction the data only weakly supported.

Trust Theory

Mayer, Davis & Schoorman, 1995

Trust as willingness to be vulnerable, based on perceived ability, benevolence, and integrity. The organizing lens for every trust dimension in the survey instrument.

04

The Application

One confirmed finding, two exploratory signals. Here's how each translates into practice — held to the same evidentiary weight it earned.

From the confirmed finding

Don't default to "younger = more receptive"

The only statistically confirmed result in this study contradicts the industry default. Any AI rollout strategy should test age-segment assumptions directly rather than inherit them.

From the exploratory signal

Treat transparency as a trust lever, not just compliance

The modest link between ethical attentiveness and desire to understand AI systems makes explainable AI (XAI) — plain-language reasons behind a recommendation — worth testing with privacy-conscious segments.

From the directional trend

Localize as a hypothesis, not a playbook

Culturally adapted AI messaging — efficiency framing in the US, heritage framing in Italy — is a reasonable A/B test, supported by qualitative interviews, but not something the survey data alone confirms.

From the process itself

Calibrated uncertainty is a deliverable

Separating "significant," "trending," and "not supported" — instead of collapsing them into one confident story — is what makes downstream recommendations trustworthy to the people who'll act on them.
